Machine Learning (ML) in Healthcare

Duration: 15 minutes Self-paced reading and 2-minute explainer video

  • Safely interpret ML-generated risk scores

What Is Machine Learning in Healthcare?

Machine Learning enables computers to learn from historical data and identify patterns without explicit programming for each decision. In healthcare, ML is commonly used for:

  • Risk estimation (e.g., deterioration, readmission, complications)
  • Supporting early identification of patients in need of closer attention
  • Prioritising resources (not for diagnosis or treatment decisions)
Key Principle

ML answers "How likely is this outcome?" while clinicians determine "What should we do about it?"

Clinical Case: Predicting Hospital Readmission
Patient Presentation
  • 72-year-old male with COPD and Type 2 Diabetes
  • Two admissions in the past 6 months; lives alone with limited support
  • On 8 regular medications
What the ML System Analyses
  • Demographics, comorbidities, medication burden
  • Admission history, social determinants
  • Vital signs and laboratory results

Each factor contributes partially, not independently.

System Output
"Predicted 30-day readmission risk: 38%"
  • This figure represents probability, not diagnosis
  • Based on large historical comparisons
  • Intended to inform care planning
What the AI Is Actually Doing
  • Learning patterns from historical datasets
  • Comparing the current patient to similar cases
  • Quantifying risk as likelihood, not certainty

ML recognises patterns, not causation.

Interpreting the 38% Risk Score
  • Does not mean the patient will definitely be readmitted
  • Indicates a higher risk compared to baseline
  • Typical bands: <15% Low; 15‚Äì30% Moderate; >30% High

Prioritise attention rather than labelling patients.

Why Clinicians Must Remain in Control
  • ML cannot assess motivation, literacy, family dynamics, cultural nuance, or bedside intuition
  • Limited by data quality, under-representation, and embedded bias
  • Automation Bias: Over-reliance on AI, undervaluing clinical judgement
Clinical Application

Steps such as earlier follow-up, community nursing, simplifying medication regimens, patient education, and multidisciplinary care can be considered.

ML supports decisions without making them.

Key Learning Summary
  • ML provides risk estimates, not outcome predictions
  • Supervised ML is the most common type
  • Risk scores guide attention rather than dictate decisions
  • Clinicians remain accountable for the final decision

What Is Deep Learning in Healthcare?

Deep Learning employs multi-layered neural networks to analyse complex data, such as:

  • Images: X-rays, CT, MRI
  • Signals: ECG, EEG
  • Audio: heart and lung sounds
  • Video: endoscopy, surgical procedures, gait analysis

Unlike traditional ML, DL learns directly from raw data, does not rely on hand-crafted features, and excels at recognising complex visual and spatial patterns.

Key Principle

DL can identify features missed by the human eye, but clinicians determine their significance.

Where DL Is Used Today

  • Chest X-ray interpretation
  • CT/MRI abnormality detection
  • Mammography screening
  • Diabetic retinopathy detection
  • ECG rhythm analysis
  • Stroke and intracranial bleed detection (decision support only)
Clinical Case: Pneumonia on Chest X-Ray
Presentation

45-year-old patient with fever (38.9¬∞C), productive cough, exertional dyspnoea; chest X-ray performed

DL Analyses

Assesses symmetry, tissue density, air bronchograms, lobar/segmental opacities, compares with normal anatomy, and evaluates spatial relationships. Analyses thousands of features simultaneously.

System Output
"High probability of lower lobe pneumonia"
Confidence level: 87% | Region highlighted
Interpreting 87% Confidence
  • This figure is not a confirmed diagnosis
  • Prompts further review by the clinician
  • Confidence does not equal certainty
Why Clinicians Must Remain Central
  • AI cannot correlate findings with patient history, may be affected by image quality/bias
  • Automation Bias: Over-trusting AI-generated heatmaps or scores
  • Guideline: Verify AI findings; do not accept them blindly
Clinical Application

Radiologist reviews AI highlights, integrates history, examination, laboratory results, and O‚ÇÇ saturation to form a diagnosis and management plan.

Additional DL Use Cases
  • Emergency department X-ray triage, CT stroke detection, mammography, retinopathy screening, wearable ECG rhythm analysis
  • Nursing: Early alerts from vital sign trends
  • Pharmacy: Adverse drug reaction risk assessment
  • Dentistry: Detection of early caries, periapical pathology, bone loss
  • Physio/Rehab: Gait and movement analysis for personalised rehabilitation plans

  • Provides outputs as confidence scores and visual cues
  • Clinicians remain responsible for decisions

What Is NLP in Healthcare?

NLP enables computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language in meaningful ways. In healthcare, NLP is focused on:

  • Clinical conversations
  • Free-text notes and referrals
  • Discharge summaries and letters
  • Patient messages and questionnaires

Unlike general speech-to-text systems, clinical NLP is designed to understand medical language, context, and intent.

Key Principle

NLP comprehends clinical meaning, not just words.

Clinical Case: Automated Clinical Documentation (GP Example)
Scenario

15-minute GP consultation for lower back pain, with NLP assisting real-time documentation.

NLP Processes
  • Converts speech to text in real time
  • Extracts key concepts (symptoms, duration, severity)
  • Interprets terminology and abbreviations in context
  • Understands relationships between symptoms, findings, diagnosis, and treatment plans
  • Structures dialogue into documentation

Computer Vision (CV) in Healthcare

Duration: 10 minutesSelf-paced reading and interactive case elements
Learning Objectives
  • Define Computer Vision within healthcare
  • Identify why CV is effective for screening and triage
  • Safely interpret CV outputs (referral recommendations)
  • Apply CV insights appropriately, retaining accountability

What Is CV in Healthcare?

Computer Vision enables computers to interpret and analyse visual information, such as images and videos. Common applications include:

  • Retinal photographs
  • Radiology (X-ray, CT, MRI)
  • Dermatology images
  • Endoscopy and surgical videos

Unlike human vision, CV systems analyse images at pixel-level resolution, can detect subtle patterns not easily visible, and apply consistent criteria across large volumes.

Key Principle

CV identifies visual patterns; clinicians determine diagnosis and management.

Clinical Case: Diabetic Retinopathy Screening
Scenario

58-year-old patient with 12-year history of Type 2 Diabetes undergoes primary care screening using a non-mydriatic camera

CV Analyses

Evaluates vessel structure and branching, microaneurysms, haemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton-wool spots, neovascularisation, and macular oedema. Analyses millions of pixels, beyond manual capacity.

System Output
"Refer to ophthalmology ‚Äì suspected proliferative changes"

Findings: Bilateral microaneurysms, haemorrhages (superior temporal), possible early neovascularisation
Recommendation: Urgent ophthalmology review within two weeks
Interpreting Safely

A referral recommendation does not guarantee inevitable vision loss, a confirmed diagnosis, or the need for immediate treatment. It indicates the presence of higher-risk features that warrant specialist review. CV is a triage tool, not a diagnostic instrument.

Clinical Application
  • Expands screening access to primary and rural settings
  • Reduces specialist workload
  • Identifies high-risk patients earlier
  • Promotes equity in areas with limited specialist resources and large diabetic populations
üß†Interactive Reflection

If image quality is poor, findings are borderline, or history is conflicting, should you repeat imaging, refer, or seek further input? This highlights the necessity of clinical oversight.

Key Benefits
  • Expands screening access
  • Consistent analysis at scale
  • Detects subtle changes
  • Supports early identification
Limitations/Safety
  • Performance depends on image quality
  • Can be affected by camera and lighting conditions
  • May underperform in rare or atypical cases
  • Requires human validation
  • Automation Bias: Assuming referral decisions are definitive
Key Reinforcement Message

CV extends access to screening, but clinicians remain responsible for final decisions.

Key Learning Summary
  • CV excels at image-based screening
  • Detects subtle visual changes at scale
  • Outputs guide triage and prioritisation
  • Clinicians retain responsibility for diagnosis and management

Rule-Based Clinical Systems

Duration: 5 minutesSelf-paced reading
Learning Objectives
  • Explain how rule-based systems operate
  • Identify strengths and limitations compared to AI systems
  • Safely apply rule-based alerts
  • Recognise the value of combining rule-based systems with AI

What Are Rule-Based Clinical Systems?

Rule-based systems are deterministic decision-support tools that use explicit, predefined rules created by experts.

Logic structure:

IF condition A AND condition B THEN perform action C

Common uses include:

  • Drug‚Äìdrug interaction checking
  • Allergy alerts
  • Contraindication warnings
  • Clinical guideline enforcement
Key Principle

Rule-based systems consistently enforce safety rules, but do not learn or adapt.

Clinical Case: Drug Interaction Alert
Scenario
  • Patient on warfarin (anticoagulant) presents with urinary tract infection
  • Clinician attempts to prescribe ciprofloxacin (antibiotic)
‚ö†Ô∏èHigh-Severity Interaction Alert ‚Äî Warfarin + Ciprofloxacin

Potential interaction: Increased risk of bleeding

Mechanism: Ciprofloxacin may increase warfarin levels

Effect: Elevated INR and bleeding risk

Recommendations: Consider alternative antibiotic; if combination is necessary, monitor INR closely, consider dose adjustment, counsel on bleeding symptoms

What the System Is Doing
  • Applies predefined IF‚ÄìTHEN logic
  • Does not learn or personalise
  • Provides deterministic output (same input yields same alert)

Predictability is both a strength and a limitation.

How Rule-Based Differs from AI

  • Rule-based: Enforces safety, is transparent and explainable, no probability or learning
  • AI (ML/DL/NLP): Estimates risk, recognises patterns, adapts over time, and supports‚Äîrather than enforces‚Äîdecisions
Key Distinction

Rule-based systems enforce safety, AI supports judgement.

Alert Fatigue

Uniform rules can lead to frequent low-value alerts, workflow interruptions, and overrides, which risk reflex dismissal and missed warnings. This is a recognised safety issue.

Modern Hybrid Approach

  • Retain critical rule-based alerts
  • Use ML to add context
  • Suppress low-value alerts
  • Preserve high-severity warnings

Improves usability without compromising safety.

Key Learning Summary
  • Fixed IF‚ÄìTHEN logic; predictable, transparent, and reliable
  • No learning or personalisation is involved
  • Best used in combination with AI to reduce alert fatigue
  • Clinicians retain accountability for decisions
