What Do We Mean by "Intelligence"?

To understand Artificial Intelligence within healthcare, it is essential to define intelligence in this context. Human intelligence in clinical practice encompasses several key abilities:

  • Gathering information through patient history, examination, and investigations
  • Recognising patterns in symptoms, signs, and clinical trends
  • Applying reasoning and drawing on professional experience
  • Making decisions in the face of uncertainty
  • Communicating effectively, showing empathy, and acting ethically
  • Accepting responsibility for outcomes

Examples:

  • A nurse identifies early patient deterioration from subtle changes in vital signs
  • A GP discerns a serious condition concealed within common symptoms
  • A physiotherapist adapts treatment based on the patient's response

This form of intelligence is shaped by context, experience, ethics, and accountability.

2

What Makes Intelligence "Artificial"?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to computer systems that perform tasks traditionally requiring human cognitive abilities. Such tasks include:

  • Recognising patterns
  • Classifying information
  • Predicting outcomes
  • Generating summaries or recommendations

AI does not replicate human thought processes. Rather, it learns statistical patterns from vast datasets and applies these patterns to new data, producing outputs instead of making definitive decisions.

Key Difference

Humans comprehend meaning, while AI detects patterns within data.

3

Artificial Intelligence in a Healthcare Context

Within healthcare, AI serves primarily as a decision-support tool, not as a decision-maker.

Capabilities of AI in Healthcare:

  • Analysing vital signs, laboratory results, images, or text
  • Flagging abnormal results
  • Estimating risk, such as patient deterioration or likelihood of readmission
  • Summarising clinical notes
  • Suggesting possible diagnoses or next steps

Limitations of AI:

  • It cannot understand the patient as an individual
  • It lacks awareness of patient values, preferences, and social context
  • Does not assume legal or ethical responsibility
  • Does not replace professional judgement
Clinical Framing

AI should be viewed as a rapid pattern-recognition assistant, entirely dependent on data quality and clinical oversight.

4

Simple Healthcare Examples

Example 1: AI in Documentation
AI does: Translates clinician speech into structured clinical notes.
Clinician does: Reviews, edits, and signs the notes, ensuring their accuracy and appropriateness.

Responsibility remains with the clinician.

Example 2: AI in Risk Prediction
AI does: Analyses vital signs, laboratory results, and trends to produce a risk score, e.g., "High risk of sepsis in next 6 hours: 72%".
Clinician does: Evaluates the patient, considers the context, and decides whether to act.

AI flags risk; clinicians make decisions.

Example 3: AI in Imaging
AI does: Highlights areas of concern on X-ray or CT scans.
Clinician does: Interprets the findings, correlates them clinically, and develops a diagnosis and management plan.

AI assists vision, but clinicians provide judgement.

5

What AI Is Not in Healthcare

Recognising the limitations of AI is crucial for ensuring safety in its use. AI is not:

  • Equivalent to human intelligence
  • Conscious or self-aware
  • Capable of empathy or moral reasoning
  • A substitute for clinical reasoning
  • Free from errors or bias
Critical Safety Point

Even if an AI system performs well on average, it may still produce incorrect results for individual patients. AI outputs must always be interpreted within the patient's context, with clinical judgement and professional accountability.

6

Key Characteristics of Healthcare AI

Data-Dependent

  • Biased data leads to biased outputs
  • Incomplete data results in unreliable outcomes
  • Even a high risk, e.g., "85% risk", implies uncertainty remains
  • Clinical interpretation is always necessary
  • AI trained in one setting (e.g., tertiary hospital) may not perform well in others (e.g., rural clinics or different populations)
  • Validation is essential
  • Healthcare AI is not "set and forget"
  • Clinical guidelines evolve, populations change, evidence develops
  • Continuous monitoring is required

Why Healthcare Is Different from Other Industries

AI is applied across many industries, but healthcare presents unique challenges and requirements.

Other industries:

  • Retail: product recommendations
  • Finance: credit scoring
  • Transport: route optimisation
  • Media: content suggestions
  • Mistakes may be inconvenient but are rarely life-threatening

Healthcare is unique:

  • Impacts human lives and vulnerable populations
  • Involves ethical obligations and legal accountability
  • Requires complex, uncertain decision-making
  • Errors can delay diagnosis, cause harm, or lead to inappropriate treatment

Key Differences:

Misconception vs Reality

‚ùå Misconception

"AI will replace doctors, nurses, and therapists."

‚úÖ Reality

AI supports professionals by managing data-intensive tasks, allowing clinicians to focus on reasoning, patient care, communication, and ethical decisions. AI should be viewed as a tool rather than a replacement for healthcare professionals.
